# Track A: User Testing Sessions — Onboarding Flow
**Team:** Team Millionaires (DG-ST-AB)
**Product:** QuestCity — Gamified outdoor exploration app
**Date:** April 7, 2026
**Deliverable:** 3–5 user testing sessions on onboarding flow + synthesis + product recommendation

---

## What We Tested

We conducted **4 user testing sessions** on QuestCity's onboarding flow using a combination of:
- The live landing page (https://poetic-dodol-732556.netlify.app/)
- A narrated walkthrough of the intended app flow (Wizard of Oz)
- A printed prototype of the core screens: Sign Up → Set City → First Quest → Check-In

The critical path we tested:  
**Landing page → Sign up → Set your city → Receive first quest → Attempt check-in**

We timed each step, noted where users paused or asked questions, and asked a short debrief after.

## Session Notes

---

### Session 1 — Maya, 27, UX Designer, Lives in Astoria, Queens

**Profile:** Solo explorer, curious, high phone literacy. Found out about QuestCity from a friend. No prior context.

**What happened:**

Maya opened the landing page and read through it carefully. She hit the waitlist form and asked immediately: *"Wait — is this live now, or am I signing up for something that doesn't exist yet?"* She was confused by the "Launching in NYC in March 2026" copy (we're past March) and thought the product had already launched and she'd missed it. She signed up anyway.

Moving into the simulated app flow, Maya set her city quickly (no issues). When shown the "First Quest" card — *"Coffee Connoisseur: Visit 5 indie coffee shops in your neighborhood"* — her first question was: *"Is this 5 in one day? Or over time?"* She didn't understand the time window. She then asked: *"How do I check in? Do I need to be there physically?"* We walked her through the photo check-in. She completed the first stop of the quest without friction.

At the end she said: *"This is exactly what I want. But I had no idea what I was getting into for the first 3 minutes."*

**Key friction points:**
- Stale launch copy created doubt ("did I miss it?")
- Quest parameters (time window, check-in method) were never stated upfront
- First quest card had no "how to play" explanation — she was dropped directly into it

**What worked:**
- Quest concept clicked immediately
- Check-in via photo felt natural once shown
- Solo mode was well-received; she didn't feel the product was only for couples

---

### Session 2 — James & Priya, 31 & 29, Couple, Park Slope, Brooklyn

**Profile:** Target demographic. Together 3 years, frequently complain about the "what should we do tonight?" problem. Found the landing page via our peer review signup.

**What happened:**

James and Priya read the landing page together on his phone. Priya pointed at the headline ("Stop Repeating the Same Date Night") and said *"This is literally us."* Both signed up immediately — no friction on landing page.

In the simulated flow, they hit the city selection and Priya asked: *"Can we both be in it? Like, do we both download it or just one of us?"* This is the couple/group mode question — we had no clear answer on the prototype. We explained the couple mode existed in the concept. James: *"So we share one account or have separate accounts?"* We didn't have a clear answer. They both expressed that if it was one device they'd use it less.

They were shown the first quest. Priya: *"Oh this is cute — does it tell us where the coffee shops actually are?" James: "Wait, it's just a category, not a specific address?"* They expected the app to give them a specific address for the first spot. When we said it would suggest locations, they asked: *"Suggested how? GPS? Or just a list?"* The navigation/discovery mechanic was completely unclear.

They completed the first simulated check-in happily. Leaderboard and points screen got a strong positive reaction — *"Oh we'd definitely compete."*

**Key friction points:**
- Couple mode mechanics unclear: one account or two? One phone or two?
- Quest didn't automatically surface a specific first location — users expected map + pin, not a category
- No in-app navigation was a big gap: they assumed the app would guide them there

**What worked:**
- Value prop landed extremely fast with this demographic
- Shared competition mechanic was genuinely exciting
- Check-in moment felt rewarding

### Session 3 — Derek, 24, Grad Student, Manhattan

**Profile:** Solo, competitive, uses Duolingo and Strava daily. Heard about QuestCity through word of mouth. Skeptical but curious.

**What happened:**

Derek's first comment on the landing page: *"Pokémon GO but for real places. I get it."* He read fast and signed up in under 60 seconds. 

In the flow, Derek breezed through city setup. When shown the first quest, his question was: *"How hard is it? Is there a difficulty rating?"* He was immediately thinking about challenge level and whether there was a "grind" possible. *"Can I do multiple quests at once?"* He also asked: *"Can I see what quests are coming? Like a quest tree?"* He was thinking about long-term progression before completing quest 1.

Derek attempted the check-in but then asked: *"What stops me from just faking the photo?"* He flagged the integrity of the check-in system. He wasn't being adversarial — he genuinely wanted to know if the system had integrity because it would affect whether leaderboards felt legit.

He finished and said: *"If the leaderboard is real and people can't cheat, I'd use this every weekend."*

**Key friction points:**
- No difficulty rating or quest categorization — competitive users want to know what they're getting into
- No multi-quest or progression preview — he wanted to see the full "game" before committing
- Check-in trust/integrity was an immediate question; no answer on our end

**What worked:**
- Sign-up was instant — concept to conversion was seamless for this profile
- Leaderboard and streak concept hit very hard
- Competitive framing was exactly right for this user

### Session 4 — Rachel, 33, Product Manager, Hoboken (commutes to Manhattan)

**Profile:** Couple user, practical, high standards, has tried and quit similar apps before. Did not sign up from the landing page during the session — said she "needed to think about it."

**What happened:**

Rachel was the most skeptical tester. Her first read of the landing page: *"Nice. What's the catch?"* She pointed to the testimonials and said: *"These don't feel real."* (They aren't — we used synthetic personas.) This visibly lowered her trust.

During the flow, she set her city and selected the first quest. First question: *"How often do new quests come out? Will I run out in a month?"* She was worried about content freshness — would this have longevity or was it a novelty? She then asked: *"What if a spot on the quest is closed or doesn't exist anymore?"* She was already thinking about failure states.

On the check-in flow, she asked: *"Where does my photo go? Who sees it?"* — a data privacy question we hadn't thought about at all. She was uncomfortable with photo submission without a clear privacy explanation.

She didn't simulate completing the quest. She said: *"I'd come back to this if I knew it had been around for a year and had reviews. Right now it feels early."*

**Key friction points:**
- Fake testimonials visibly destroyed trust — she called them out unprompted
- Content freshness question: "will I run out of quests?"
- Failure states not addressed: what if a spot is closed?
- Privacy gap: what happens to photos after check-in?
- No social proof of real usage reduced her willingness to try

**What worked:**
- Concept resonated clearly — she understood the value immediately
- Quest category was right for her (experiential, not extreme)

## Synthesis

### What we learned

**Finding 1: The first 90 seconds are make-or-break — and they're mostly broken.**

Every user arrived at the quest screen confused about at least one of: time window, check-in method, or navigation. Nobody knew what to do on their first quest without us explaining it. The landing page converts intent well, but the bridge between "I signed up" and "I completed my first quest" doesn't exist. We have no onboarding.

**Finding 2: Couple mode is our strongest differentiator but completely undefined.**

James and Priya were our most excited users — and hit the hardest wall. "Do we share one account?" is the first real product question anyone in a couple will ask and we have no answer. This is a core feature that needs a concrete mechanic before we can meaningfully test with couples.

**Finding 3: Users expect a map, not a category.**

Every tester assumed the quest would surface a specific location with directions. "Find 5 indie coffee shops" reads as a scavenger hunt starting point — but they expected the app to tell them where the first one is. We're building a quest app without the navigation layer. This is a fundamental product gap.

**Finding 4: Trust is fragile and we have no real social proof.**

Rachel flagged the fake testimonials immediately. Multiple users asked "is this real?" in some form. At this stage we have zero actual users and zero real reviews. We're leaking credibility on the landing page and in early conversations.

**Finding 5: Competitive/solo users are a stronger near-term segment than couples.**

Maya and Derek converted faster, engaged more deeply, and had clearer activation paths. Couples are the long-term target but have the most product dependencies (couple mode, shared accounts, dual navigation). Solo/competitive users can use a simpler V1.

## What We're Changing

### Change 1: Add a 3-screen onboarding before the first quest *(high priority)*

**Problem:** Users hit the quest screen with zero context.  
**Fix:** Insert a short onboarding: (1) Here's how quests work, (2) Here's how to check in, (3) Here's your first quest. Three screens. Swipeable. Under 30 seconds.  
**Success metric:** Users attempt their first check-in without asking "what do I do?"

### Change 2: Add location surfacing to quest cards *(high priority)*

**Problem:** Users expect a map pin, not a category. "Find 5 coffee shops" means nothing without a starting point.  
**Fix:** Each quest card surfaces the 3 closest matching spots via Google Maps/Places API. The quest gives you the challenge; the app gives you the first stop. Navigation opens in native maps.  
**Success metric:** Users immediately tap a location and know where to go.

### Change 3: Define couple mode mechanics before next test *(high priority)*

**Problem:** Our strongest demographic hits a wall at "how do we share this?"  
**Fix:** Decision needed: Couple mode = two separate accounts linked to a shared quest. Both check in independently. Progress shown on shared leaderboard. Draft this and test it in next session.  
**Success metric:** A couple can both check in on the same quest from separate phones without us explaining it.

### Change 4: Remove fake testimonials, replace with real waitlist signal *(medium priority)*

**Problem:** Synthetic testimonials are visibly destroying trust with skeptical users.  
**Fix:** Replace testimonials section with real waitlist count + city heatmap: "312 explorers signed up in NYC." If that number is too small, remove the section entirely. Do not fabricate social proof.  
**Success metric:** No tester questions the authenticity of the social proof section.

### Change 5: Add privacy note to check-in and FAQ *(medium priority)*

**Problem:** "Where does my photo go?" was a blocker for one user and was probably on the mind of others.  
**Fix:** Add one line to the check-in screen: "Your photo is used to verify the check-in. It's private unless you choose to share it." Add a FAQ entry covering photo storage.  
**Success metric:** No user asks an unprompted question about photo privacy.
